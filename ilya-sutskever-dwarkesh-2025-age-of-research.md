## What is a value function?

**Dwarkesh:** It might be worth defining for the audience what a value function is, if you want to.

**Ilya:** Sure. When people do reinforcement learning the way it's done right now — how do they train these agents? You have your neural net, you give it a problem, and you tell the model, "Go solve it." The model takes maybe thousands or hundreds of thousands of actions or thoughts, and then produces a solution. The solution is scored, and that score is used to provide a training signal for every single action in your trajectory. That means that if you're doing something that goes on for a long time — a task that takes a long time to solve — you do no learning at all until you come up with a proposed solution. That's how reinforcement learning is done naively; that's how o1 and R1 are ostensibly done.

The value function says something like: maybe I could sometimes — not always — tell you if you're doing well or badly before the end. The notion is more useful in some domains than others. For example, when you play chess and you lose a piece, you know you messed up. You don't need to play the whole game to know that what you just did was bad, and therefore whatever preceded it was also bad. So the value function lets you short-circuit the wait until the very end. Suppose you're doing some kind of math or programming thing, exploring a particular solution direction, and after, say, a thousand steps of thinking you conclude that this direction is unpromising. As soon as you conclude that, you could already get a reward signal a thousand time steps earlier, when you decided to pursue this path. You say, "Next time I shouldn't pursue this path in a similar situation," long before you actually came up with a proposed solution.

**Dwarkesh:** This was in the DeepSeek R1 paper — that the space of trajectories is so wide that maybe it's hard to learn a mapping from an intermediate trajectory to a value. Also, in coding, for example, you'll have the wrong idea, then go back, then change something.

**Ilya:** This sounds like such a lack of faith in deep learning. Sure, it might be difficult — but nothing deep learning can't [handle]. My expectation is that value functions should be useful, and I fully expect they will be used in the future, if not already.

What I was alluding to with the person whose emotional center got damaged is that maybe the human value function is modulated by emotions in some important way that's hardcoded by evolution — and maybe that's important for people to be effective in the world.

## "AGI" and "pre-training" as misleading words; continual learning

**Ilya:** I'll give you another example of how language affects thinking — in this case, two words that have shaped everyone's thinking. First word: AGI. Second word: pre-training.

Why does the term "AGI" exist? It's a very particular term. The reason it exists, in my opinion, is not so much because it's an essential descriptor of some end state of intelligence, but because it's a reaction to a different term that existed: narrow AI. If you go back to the ancient history of game-play AI — checkers AI, chess AI, computer-games AI — everyone would say, "Look at this narrow intelligence. Sure, the chess AI can beat Kasparov, but it can't do anything else. It is so narrow — artificial narrow intelligence." In reaction, some people said, "This is not good, it's so narrow; what we need is general AI — an AI that can do all the things." And that term got a lot of traction.

The second thing that got a lot of traction is pre-training — specifically, the recipe of pre-training. (I think the way people do RL now is maybe undoing the conceptual imprint of pre-training.) Pre-training had the property that you do more of it and the model gets better at everything, more or less uniformly. General AI; pre-training gives AGI. But the thing that happened with "AGI" and pre-training is that, in some sense, they overshot the target. Because if you think about the term AGI — especially in the context of pre-training — you'll realize that a human being is not an AGI. Yes, there's a foundation of skills, but a human being lacks a huge amount of knowledge. Instead, we rely on continual learning.

So suppose we achieve success and produce some kind of safe superintelligence. The question is: how do you define it? Where on the curve of continual learning is it going to be? I produce a super-intelligent 15-year-old that's very eager to go — they don't know very much at all, a great student, very eager — and you say, "Go be a programmer, go be a doctor, go and learn." You could imagine that the deployment itself will involve some kind of learning, trial-and-error period.

**Dwarkesh:** It's a process, as opposed to dropping the finished thing. So you're suggesting that the thing you're pointing at with superintelligence is not some finished mind that knows how to do every single job in the economy — because the way the original OpenAI charter defines AGI is that it can do every single thing a human can do. You're proposing instead a mind that can *learn* to do every single job. And that is superintelligence. Once you have the learning algorithm, it gets deployed into the world the same way a human laborer might join an organization.

## How evolution encodes high-level desires

**Dwarkesh:** I wonder if the fact that emotions — developed millions, in many cases billions, of years ago in a totally different environment — are still guiding our actions so strongly is an example of alignment success. I feel strongly that they're baked in, and I don't know how evolution did it, because it's a high-level concept represented in the brain. What people think — say you care about some social thing — it's not a low-level signal like smell, for which there's a sensor. **Dwarkesh:** What's especially impressive: if it were a desire you learned in your lifetime, it kind of makes sense, because your brain is intelligent. **Ilya:** Essentially. Or, put differently: think about the tools available to the genome. It says, "Here's a recipe for building a brain." ## Self-play

**Dwarkesh:** I've heard you hint in the past about self-play as a way to either get data or match agents to other agents of equivalent intelligence to kick off learning. How should we think about why there are no public proposals of this kind of thing working with LLMs?

**Ilya:** Two things. The reason I thought self-play was interesting is that it offered a way to create models using compute only, without data. If you think data is the ultimate bottleneck, then using compute only is very interesting. That's what makes it interesting. Now, the thing is that self-play — at least the way it was done in the past, where you have agents that compete with each other — is only good for developing a certain set of skills. It's too narrow. It's only good for negotiation, conflict, certain social skills, strategizing, that kind of stuff. So if you care about those skills, self-play will be useful.

Actually, I think self-play did find a home, just in a different form. Things like debate, prove-a-verifier, having an LLM as a judge that's also incentivized to find mistakes in your work — you could say this is not exactly self-play, but it's a related adversarial setup that people are doing. And really, self-play is a special case of more general competition between agents. The natural response to competition is to try to be different. If you put multiple agents together and tell them, "You all need to work on some problem," and you're an agent inspecting what everyone else is working on, you'll say, "Well, if they've already taken this approach, it's not clear I should pursue it; I should pursue something differentiated." So something like this could also create an incentive for a diversity of approaches.

Some examples. The idea of the artificial neuron is directly inspired by the brain, and it's a great idea. Why? You say, sure, the brain has all these different organs, it has the faults — but the faults probably don't matter. Why do we think the neurons matter? Because there are many of them. It kind of feels right. So you want the neuron. You want some kind of local learning rule that will change the connections between the neurons — it feels plausible that the brain does it. The idea of distributed representation; the idea that the neural network should learn from experience, because the brain learns from experience. You ask yourself: is something fundamental or not fundamental, how things should be? I think that's been guiding me a fair bit — thinking from multiple angles and looking for almost beauty, simplicity, elegance, correctness, inspiration from the brain. There's no room for ugliness. All of those things need to be present at the same time, and the more they are present, the more confident you can be in a top-down belief.

And then the top-down belief is the thing that sustains you when the experiments contradict you. Because if you just trust the data all the time — well, sometimes you can be doing a correct thing, but there's a bug, and you don't know there's a bug. How can you tell there's a bug? How do you know if you should keep debugging or conclude it's the wrong direction? It's the top-down belief: "Things have to be this way. Something like this has to work. Therefore, we've got to keep going." And it's based on this multifaceted beauty and inspiration by the brain.
