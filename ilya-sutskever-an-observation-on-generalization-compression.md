## Kolmogorov complexity as the ultimate compressor

…[With an] unsupervised learning algorithm you can say whether it's the first case or the second case: "I know I've done my best at benefiting from my unlabeled data, and no one could have done better than me."

Now I want to take a detour to theory-land, which is a little obscure, but I think it's interesting: Kolmogorov complexity as the ultimate compressor gives us the ultimate low-regret algorithm — which is actually not an algorithm, because it's not computable, but you'll see what I mean really quickly.

For some context: who here is familiar with Kolmogorov complexity? Okay, about 50%. It's the kind of thing that's easy to explain in one minute, so I'll just do it. Imagine you give me some data, and I'm going to compress it by giving you the shortest program that can possibly exist — the shortest program which, if you run it, outputs your data. [Audience member confirms.] Yes, that's correct — it is the length of the shortest program which outputs X.

Intuitively, you can see that this compressor is quite good, because you can prove a theorem that's also really easy to prove — or rather, it's easy to *feel*, and once you feel it, you can believe me that it's easy to prove. You can basically say that if you use your Kolmogorov compressor to compress your strings, you'll have very low regret about your compression. The result says that if you've got your string X — your dataset, database, whatever — the shortest program which outputs X is shorter than whatever your compressor output (however well your compressor compressed your data), plus a little term: however many characters of code you need to implement your compressor.

Intuitively, you can see how it makes sense — it's a simulation argument. If you tell me, "Hey, I've got this really great compressor C," I'm going to say, "Cool — does it come with a computer program? Can you give this program to K?" And K is going to run your compressor, because it runs computer programs; you just need to pay for the program length. So without giving you the details, I think I've given you the feel of it: the Kolmogorov compressor can simulate other computer programs, simulate other compressors. This is also why it's not computable — it feels very much at liberty to simulate all computer programs. But it is the best compressor that exists.

## From Kolmogorov complexity to neural networks

We were talking about good compression for unsupervised learning. Now let us generalize the Kolmogorov compressor to be allowed to use side information. I'll reiterate this point several times because it's important: obviously, the Kolmogorov compressor is not computable, it's undecidable, because it searches over all programs. But hey — did you know that if you run SGD over the parameters of some neural net with 100 layers, it's automatically doing program search over a computer which has a certain amount of memory and a certain number of steps? It's like a micro-micro-micro version of it. With a neural net, you kind of feel the similarity. It's kind of magical, right? Neural networks can simulate little programs — they're little computers, they're circuits, and circuits are computing machines. SGD searches over the program, and all of deep learning hinges on the SGD miracle: that we can actually train these computers with SGD, that it works to actually find those circuits from data. Therefore we can compute our miniature Kolmogorov compressor, and the simulation argument applies here as well.

By the way, I just want to mention one fact. If you've ever tried to design a better neural network architecture, you'd find that it's kind of hard. You say, "Let's add this connection, let's modify that." Why is it hard? The simulation argument: your new architecture can be pretty straightforwardly simulated by the old architecture — except when it can't. Those are rare cases, and in those rare cases you have a big improvement, such as when you switch from the little RNN to the Transformer. The RNN has a bottleneck — the hidden state — so it has a hard time implementing the Transformer. However, had we found a way to engineer an RNN with a very, very large state, perhaps it would become as good as a Transformer again. So you start to see how we switch from the formal land to neural-network land — you see the similarity.

## Conditional Kolmogorov complexity as the solution to unsupervised learning

Conditional Kolmogorov complexity is the solution to unsupervised learning. You can have a similar theorem. Let me define what K(Y | X) is: it's the length of the shortest program which outputs Y if it's allowed to probe X. You can prove the same result, and you can see immediately that this is, definitionally, the solution to unsupervised learning. If you use that, you can sleep very soundly at night knowing that no one does unsupervised learning better than you. It's literally that — this is the ultimate low-regret solution to unsupervised learning, except that it's not computable. But I do think it's a useful framework. Here we condition on a dataset, not an example; this thing will extract all the value out of X for predicting Y — the dataset, not the example.

There's one little technicality I need to spend a bit of time on. We were talking about conditional Kolmogorov complexity, where you try to compress one thing while having access to another thing. This is a bit unnatural in a machine learning context, where you care about fitting big datasets — at least as of today, although it's changing fairly rapidly. But I think it's still fair to say that there's no truly good way of conditioning on a big dataset. You can *fit* a big dataset, but you can't *condition* on it — not yet, not truly.

So this next result says: if you care about making predictions about your supervised task, then using the good old-fashioned Kolmogorov compressor — which just compresses the concatenation of X and Y — is going to be just as good as using your conditional compressor. There are more details and subtleties to what I just said, and I'm happy to talk about them offline. But it basically says that, whereas a previous slide said you can use the conditional Kolmogorov compressor to solve unsupervised learning, this says you can also use your regular Kolmogorov compressor: just throw all your data together, take all your files, concatenate them, compress them — and that's going to make great predictions on the supervised task you care about. There are some intuitions about why this is true; proving it is slightly more tricky, so I won't do it. So: the solution to unsupervised learning is to just give it all to your Kolmogorov compressor.

The final thing I'll mention is that this kind of joint compression is maximum likelihood, as long as you don't overfit. If you have a dataset, the sum of the likelihoods given your parameters is the cost of compressing the dataset — you also need to pay the cost of compressing the parameters. And if you now want to compress two datasets, no problem: just add more points to your dataset, adding the terms to the sum. So this joint compression is very natural in a machine learning context, which is why it was worth the hassle of going through the conditional Kolmogorov complexity first. It's elegant — I like it — because it says that if you squint hard enough, this explains what neural networks are doing. SGD over big neural networks is our big program search; bigger neural networks approximate the Kolmogorov compressor more and more, better and better. Maybe this is also why we like big neural nets: because we approach the unapproachable ideal of the Kolmogorov compressor, which truly has no regret, and we just want to have less and less regret as we train larger and larger neural nets — as far as extracting predictive value is concerned.

## Does this apply to GPT models?

Now, the way this applies to the GPT models — I claim this theory applies to them too. The thing that's a little tricky with the GPT models is that the theory of their behavior can also be explained without making any reference to compression or unsupervised learning. You just say: it's the conditional distribution of text on the internet. Few-shot learning — just imagine a document with some repeating patterns; the pattern is probably going to continue. So the GPT models' few-shot behavior can definitely be explained without alluding to this theory.

So I thought it would be nice to find some other direct validation of this theory. Can we find a different domain, like vision — because in vision you have pixels — and show that doing this on pixels leads to good unsupervised learning? The answer is yes.

## iGPT: validating the theory in the image domain

This is work we did in 2020 called iGPT. It's an expensive proof of concept — it was not meant to be a practical procedure; it was meant to be a paper that showed that if you have a really good next-step predictor, you're going to do great on unsupervised value, proven in the image domain. You take your image, turn it into a sequence of pixels, give each pixel some discrete value of intensity, and then just do next-pixel prediction with the same Transformer. That's it — different from BERT; just next-token prediction, because this maximizes the likelihood and therefore compresses.

We saw one immediate result. These are results on CIFAR-10: you have models of different sizes, with their next-step prediction accuracy on the pixel-prediction (unsupervised) task on one axis, and linear-probe accuracy on the other. (A linear probe is when you pick some layer inside your neural net, fit the best linear classifier on it, and see how well it does.) You get these nice-looking curves, and they start to get more and more similar — which is what you want. It's working: next-pixel prediction, of the same variety as next-word prediction — not mere pixel prediction, but *next*-pixel prediction — leads to better and better unsupervised learning. I thought that was pretty cool.

We scaled it up to various degrees and showed that it indeed learns reasonably well. We came close but didn't fully match the gap between the best unsupervised learning and the best supervised learning of the day on ImageNet — but it clearly did feel like it scaled, and that it was a matter of compute. These things use large, high-resolution images, and we used tiny 64×64 images with transformers that were giant for the day — six billion parameters, tiny by today's standards. So: you do unsupervised next-pixel prediction on a big image dataset, fit your linear probe on ImageNet, and get strong results. That's cool. On CIFAR-10, one of the cool things — we got 99% on CIFAR-10 with this, which was cool. 2020 was a different time, but it was cool.

## Linear representations: an open mystery

I'll close with a couple of comments on linear representations. I like the compression theory because it bothered me for a long time that you couldn't think rigorously about unsupervised learning. I claim now you can, at least to some partial degree — still lots of hand-waving is needed, but maybe a bit less than before. But it doesn't say *why* your representations should be linearly separable; it doesn't say a linear probe should work. Linear representations happen all the time, and the reason for it must be deep and profound. I think we might be able to crisply articulate it at some point.

One thing I thought was interesting: these next-pixel-prediction autoregressive models seem to have better linear representations than BERT — the blue [bars] are BERT versus autoregressive. I'm not sure why that is. I can speculate, but it would be nice to gain more understanding of why those linear representations are formed. And that's the end. Thank you for your attention. [Applause]

## Q&A

**Audience:** Could you provide the speculation?

**Ilya:** Yeah. The speculation is basically this. If you're doing next-pixel prediction, you're predicting the next pixel from all the previous pixels, so you need to look at the long-range structure. But in BERT, you have your vector and you drop, say, 25% of the tokens (the pixels, in this case); then any prediction you have can actually be done quite well by looking a little bit into the past and a little bit into the future. So you remove all the hard prediction tasks, or you make them much easier. The hardest prediction task in next-pixel prediction is a lot harder than the hardest prediction task in the BERT case. This is an argument — we might even be able to test it if we design an experiment for it. But that's the speculation.

**Audience:** Is there a more robust 2D version of next-pixel prediction?

**Ilya:** I would say anything you do at all that turns a neural network into a probabilistic model — that assigns probabilities to different inputs — works. The other big way of doing next-token prediction is diffusion models. The diffusion models people use in high-quality image generators don't really maximize the likelihood of their inputs; they have a different objective. But the most original formulation does maximize likelihood. And by the way, the diffusion model is a counterargument to my [theory] — rather, I would claim the diffusion model should also have worse representations than a next-token-prediction model, for the same reason that BERT does.
