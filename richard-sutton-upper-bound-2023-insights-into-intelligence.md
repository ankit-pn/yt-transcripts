## Introduction: the narratives of AI

As I think you know, I'm a sort of dedicated AI researcher, but I'm also a bit of a philosopher — I like to think — and an observer of human nature and the human condition, as I think we all are. So I appreciate this chance to speak to you all and share my views about AI and about the field — all that's happening, all the growth, all the change, all the excitement, all the hyperbole, all the fear, the positive and negative exaggeration. It's sort of hard to get a coherent, consistent, reliable, stable view, and I just wanted to talk about all these things today.

So today I'm not going to talk about whatever appeared on the schedule — that was sort of a placeholder. AI narratives. "Narrative" just means a story, so this is the stories of AI: what we tell each other, what we tell ourselves, and how to think about them — which ones are accurate, which may be mistaken, how they may change over time, and how the future might work out.

But let me be clear at the outset: I'm really optimistic. I think the future will be very, very bright. It's not going to be a utopia — nothing like that; good things and bad things will still happen — but mostly good things. AI won't even really change that. There will still be good things and bad things; it may change *which* good things happen and *which* bad things happen, but overall I think AI will make the world a better place. It'll make it more interesting, more prosperous, more dynamic, more challenging in a good way.

## The root of it all: Moore's law and the age of computation

Okay, I've got a gizmo — let's start. I want to start with the root of it all, the cause, and the cause, I think, is Moore's law — what we colloquially call Moore's law. For this century, and the coming decades, computation has become cheaper and cheaper, more and more plentiful, and this is really the story of our age. This is the age of computation.

Let's look at this graph. Across the bottom we've got years, and plotted on the y-axis is the computational power of computers available in that year. Notice the years span over a century — I go back to the beginning of 1900 — and the y-axis is a log scale, so each tick up is a factor. The big marks are two orders of magnitude — a factor of 100 for each mark on the y-axis. The amazing thing is that over this period of time — roughly a century — computer power has doubled roughly every two years. A little faster recently, maybe now it's 18 months, but it's been remarkably constant and remarkably consistent over all that time.

Can you see, for example, World War II anywhere in that graph? I would say not at all — not a blip. No world war, no single technological event [shows up]; it's amazingly consistent. Over the 80 or 90 years since computers were invented, there's been an increase of 10 to the 16th. Ten to the 16th — that's a hundred thousand million million. It's unimaginable what can happen cumulatively over this kind of timeframe when you get this consistent doubling. It just keeps happening. This is the story of our age. This is truly the age of computation. This is the root of it all.

## Is it AI, or is it computation?

But let's think a little more analytically. Is this AI, or is this computation — or is computation AI? How do we use these words? I think these two things are confounded in our minds and in our language: lots of computation, and being related to intelligence.

"AI" as a name has cachet — it's appealing — and so we're drawn to it. We want to call whatever we're doing AI. I was in the drugstore the other day looking at electric toothbrushes, and what did I find but AI toothbrushes? It says it right there at the top. [Laughter] This is the way our language evolves, and it's not a new thing — it's always been true. When I was in graduate school, we didn't have our own computer; we had a terminal that connected to the big computer, and the exciting thing was to get an *intelligent* terminal. It was "intelligent" just because it could do multiple fonts and a little bit of editing.

Now to less silly, real things of today: we have AlphaFold. AlphaFold is this amazing program from DeepMind, where they figured out the three-dimensional structures of all the proteins known to man from their sequences. It's really transformed biology — super important and super exciting — and it is exactly the use of computation to do something really, really hard. But I would say it is really *computation*. They use neural networks, machine learning — but they're not like a person, not like an intelligent system that has a goal. Anyway, we call that AI, and everyone's kind of good with that: these massively complex and powerful new tools, we call them AI. That may be a misnomer, but everyone's doing it, and who am I? I'm not the kind of guy who would insist on using words differently from everyone else. Thank you for that laugh.

## Tool AI vs. agent AI

So I'm going to say controversial things, but I don't want to start with a trivial one like how we use words. Let's go with the words — let's call this AI. But let's make a distinction between two kinds of AI. **Tool AI**, where we use massive computation to do something people find hard — but these tools have to be wielded by a person; they are not autonomous, not complete, not fully powerful on their own. They're powerful in conjunction with a person. As opposed to **agent AI**: if tool AI is creating crazy-smart tools, then agent AI is creating crazy-smart artificial *people* — new people.

So I've got two dimensions. One dimension is tool AI versus agent AI, and this is how I'll try to add something to the conversation about our narrative for AI. First we divide it into these two parts, and then we divide it into the positive and negative stories and exaggerations around each. I think it's really helpful to separate these two, because we often lump them together, and that causes us to think in a sloppy way. So maybe that's my contribution: to separate them.

Examples of tool AI would be AlphaFold and large language models — they're not full agents, but they're useful tools. The image generators like DALL·E and Midjourney don't have goals, don't have an interaction, don't really have a life; they do things and are wielded by a person as tools. On the other side, we have agent AI — the AlphaGos and AlphaZeros, DQN, the Atari players, GT Sophy (the race-car agent many of us heard about the other day). Those are agents. Even simple grid-world systems can be agents. So we can make this distinction based on whether they have clear goals and whether they have experience — whether they have agency.

You may remember, from the Alberta Plan, we have a picture of the agent: it has perception, a reactive policy, a value function, a model of the world. All those things are not in the tools — the tools are not about aspiring to create a whole agent. I think this is a really useful distinction. Maybe we can find edge cases where it's not so clear, but let's go with it.

## Tool AI: positive and negative

Let's spend a while on tool AI. It has a positive and a negative part. The negative part feels dangerous, threatening — things like fake news, deepfakes, election manipulation, and loss of jobs (or rather, *changing* jobs). There are all these negative things we're familiar with, because they happen every time we have a new technology that disrupts old jobs. So this is negative and threatening — but at the same time it's familiar; it feels like not a big deal. We've seen it happen many times before, and at least in the past, more and better jobs were created to make up for the ones that were lost. So this is a story that's slightly threatening, but so familiar that it's not super scary. The really scary stuff comes from the agent side.

## Will tools spontaneously become agents?

Now, the next thing: with this tool stuff, could we end up with agent stuff? This is a story people tell, particularly about large language models — that they're getting so smart, they understand things, that they're going to become agents, powerful and threatening in a way that's beyond the normal. I want to diss that idea a little bit, and the best way is through humor. I love this cartoon: *"To think this all began with letting autocomplete finish our sentences."* I love it because it has both exaggerations in it: the exaggeration that if we let autocomplete go, it'll end up being agent AI, a powerful thing that will dominate and control us — and it's a *negative* exaggeration, because "if they become agents, they will harm us, they will be our overlords." It's a cartoon, not an argument, but it's a good, fun aside.

I still tend to dismiss the idea that we'll have a spontaneous evolution from large language models into agents, mainly because I've spent so much of my life trying to make agents, and I know it doesn't happen just by chance or by throwing in computation. You have to really organize them, structure them, and try to make them powerful agents for that to happen. Of course, I could be wrong.

Now another reason I doubt it — another weak argument, so take it as an interesting one, not definitive. We see a psychological tendency: people thinking about complicated things grab hold of one thing and suggest that maybe that's the *only* important thing. (Maybe I did that today — I hope not.) I'll give you some examples. This is what happened with neural networks and gradient descent — gradient descent is a powerful idea, and many people ran with it so hard that they tried to say maybe that's all we need, leaving out, for example, the ideas of reinforcement learning and control. Other people got psyched on prediction as a very important part of cognition — and I totally agree — but then they'd go too far and say *all* cognition is just prediction. This is sort of what we're seeing with large language models. They've shown to be powerful, so many people want them to be the one idea that gives everything. Ascribing godlike potential to one thing is a common, flawed way of thinking.

And let me be clear: large language models are great, extremely useful — but they are the premier example of *positive exaggeration* of the capabilities of current AI systems, in my opinion. I think they will disappoint a bit, but they'll also be extremely useful. Let's just try to be reasonable. Again, a weak argument — interesting but not definitive.

## The ELIZA effect

Let me give you another weak argument — these help us gain perspective. A sociological argument: the whole phenomenon of tremendous excitement around large language models is very familiar to AI researchers, at least old ones like myself. We'd remember something called ELIZA. Wikipedia has the "ELIZA effect": if there are systems that generate words and appear to speak to us, we will over-interpret them. This came up in the 1960s — they had simple programs that used language, and when people, even smart people, physicists and scientists, interacted with them, they would read a lot of agency and understanding into the systems. It's a reasonable effect, well known, and undoubtedly part of why large language models are so exciting and have such possibility for hyperbole.

Here's what ELIZA looked like — remember, it's from the '60s, so it has a terminal interface. Let me read you an example conversation, to give you a sense of what this was. ELIZA was pretending to be a psychiatrist. ELIZA says to the person, "Is something troubling you?" The person says, "Men are all alike." ELIZA: "What is the connection, do you suppose?" The person: "They're always bugging us about something or other." ELIZA: "Can you think of a specific example?" It seems like ELIZA is really understanding — but it also seems quite possible this is a canned response: when someone uses the word "always," a safe thing to say is "Can you think of a specific example?" The human says, "Well, my boyfriend made me come here," and ELIZA says, "Is it important to you that your boyfriend made you come here?" Again — is it understanding, or just parroting back the phrase you gave it? It's a really simple program. This is the 1960s — that's 60 years, a factor of a million million in computation; this was a tiny computer.

The ELIZA effect, I think, is still with us. It's easy for us to read too much intelligence and understanding into systems that talk. Those are my weak arguments — context and background for understanding large language models and how to assess their significance going forward.

## Agent AI: understanding intelligence as the grand challenge

Now let's turn to agent AI. Here I want to remind you of a powerful truth: a genuine understanding of intelligence would be a very big deal. For thousands of years, philosophers and ordinary people alike have wondered about and sought to understand human intelligence. Almost every great philosopher since Plato has devoted a major part of their work to the philosophy of mind. Some familiar memes: John Locke wrote *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* — obviously about intelligence and mind; Immanuel Kant wrote the *Critique of Pure Reason*; and of course René Descartes famously said, "I think, therefore I am." This is something we talk about in AI — it's techy, it's computers — but the challenge of understanding intelligence, of making agent AI, is really this old, old goal of all of humanity and science.

After the philosophers came more like scientists, and people have always been fascinated by their inner workings: Gustav Fechner did studies in psychophysics, along with Ebbinghaus; then psychologists and animal-learning theorists like Pavlov, Thorndike, Skinner, and Tolman; and a different kind of science with people like Jean Piaget, Sigmund Freud, Carl Jung, even Timothy Leary. They're all wondering: how do our minds work, and how can we make them work better? This is a grand challenge, a great mystery, and our interest in it is not just narcissism — well, it *is* kind of narcissism, focusing on ourselves and how we work, but it's appropriate, because intelligence is a great, powerful thing.

I think Ray Kurzweil was not wrong when he said that intelligence is the most powerful phenomenon in the universe. More powerful than what — supernovas, black holes? Is that crazy, or is that right? Black holes and supernovas are pretty powerful, but intelligence over time… the supernova had maybe a billion years to develop; give intelligence a billion years and see what it can do. Maybe we'll end up moving the stars around even more than a supernova.

So understanding intelligence is a great challenge that would change all of our lives in too many ways to predict. To understand how intelligence works is the Holy Grail of science and philosophy; to achieve such an understanding would be perhaps the greatest scientific achievement of any age.

## Is it good? Creating new people

I have a sort of quiz to help us get started. Could AI be such a powerful phenomenon? I've already told you I'd say yes — it could be the most powerful phenomenon. Now ask: can *tool* AI be such a powerful phenomenon? I'd say no. The powerful phenomenon is the people wielding the tools; the tools themselves are not powerful.

This is a long-standing goal of humankind: to understand how we think, improve ourselves, and even use technology to create new beings — or to *become* new beings — as intelligent and powerful as we are now. It's still being driven by Moore's law; it's happening now. Pursuing the prize is a great and glorious thing to do. A shot at the prize is much more important than personal fortune or a contribution to the economy. All the world's economy is small compared to this kind of transition in the status of the world, the planet, this portion of the universe. How would we not want to be part of it?

So I'm giving you the positive story — why it's exciting, why this is the natural next step. But I really want to phrase the question for you, not just present my point of view. The key question is: *is it good?* As I dwelled on last year, often we don't get to choose these things, but we can still evaluate them, still say whether they're good or bad — and that's a very important part of the narrative we tell.

So focus on this: creating new people who are smarter than we are now — is this a grand and glorious prize, a natural next step in the human quest? Or is it a nightmare, bringing the end of all we know and love? Would it be good, or bad? I want you to recognize that it's hard to assess such a thing — it's very personal, very subjective — and I don't really want to answer it today. I want to phrase the question, make it prominent on your board of things happening in AI, and have you think about it.

You can tell I'm on the grand-and-glorious side. Others would say, "Look at Rich, he's an AI scientist, he's lusting for the prize, the fame, the glory." And I would say to those people: they're just fearing change, fearing they'd lose control of a world they don't even really control now. They're talking themselves into an unwarranted fear, and it's sort of Messianic — because if you think you're going to save the world, you may do drastic and even evil things.

## The fear-monger narrative: control and alignment

So these are our narratives: the fearful narrative and the hopeful one. The fear-monger narrative, I'm sad to say, I think is winning a bit. It's the standard. Everyone knows: "AI — potential dangers, a bunch of potential good things, but we all know it's potential dangers." The first sophistication I'm trying to add is to separate tool AI from agent AI. I'm not saying agent AI isn't dangerous — maybe it's the thing that's potentially *more* dangerous — but right now we have only one narrative for agent AI: that it's dangerous, we have to control it, keep it from getting out of control. We should have another narrative.

One way to see this: the standard, recognized thing to do in the field of AI is to solve the **control problem** — how do you control the AIs? It's not unlike a slaveholder saying, "How do I control my slaves? They're getting more intelligent, figuring stuff out; how do I make sure they're never out of control?" The standard field doesn't ask whether that slaveholder point of view is appropriate, doesn't ask whether people would use their AIs against each other, doesn't ask whether this is moral.

Also consider that the AI-safety point of view tries to solve the **alignment problem** — seizing control of all the agents in the world and ensuring their goals are aligned with those of people. But it doesn't ask: *which* people? People have different goals. It doesn't ask how that would be enforced, or what kind of world you'd have if no one could make an AI that wasn't approved by something. Wouldn't this freeze things in place? How could our values evolve? Our values are not perfect; the world of people has many flaws, and we need to be challenged and to continue to evolve. The fact that we don't ask these difficult questions — that's my evidence that the fear-monger narrative is winning. We stop at "we have to control it, we have to align it," and don't go on to see the obvious challenges to that way of thinking.

## Where the fear comes from: fear of the other tribe

Where does this fear come from? Why are we so viscerally fearful of the potential of strong agent AI? I think it's really in our DNA, in our instinctual history. I call it the **fear of the other tribe**. Many times in humankind's history, different peoples have come into contact; sometimes it's peaceful — trade, intermarriage — and sometimes it's violent. I'd say more often it's violent: one group or the other dominates, kills, enslaves. This is in our genetic history, both as the dominator and the dominated, and these attitudes are still part of our attitudes toward others. Now, particularly in Canada, we try to be more open-minded, to embrace differences, to be welcoming and not fearful — but the fear-monger narrative of AI builds on our instinctual fear of the other tribe, the other people who are strong. And we know from our history that there are major advantages if we can overcome that and work together and collaborate.

## The hopeful picture: allies and offspring

Now I want to paint a more positive picture to counterbalance the fearful narrative. The hopeful picture is that the AIs are not alien things to be controlled, but our allies and our offspring — they are *of* us rather than *against* us. As such, we don't try to control them tightly out of fear; rather, we appreciate the differences, work with them, and arrange our society so that we all see it as beneficial to work together — including the possibility that they might teach us something fundamental, just as we expect that our children, as they grow up, might teach us something fundamental. We don't just train our children; we also learn from them.

So in the hopeful narrative, we don't ask, "How can we control the AIs' goals?" but rather, "How can we arrange society so that they will want to work together, even though they have different goals?" Notice this is already how we do it with people. People have many different goals — we say "human values," but really everyone has different goals. Your food is not my food; we have different stomachs, different families, different bank accounts. It's not that our goals are the same; in a real sense they're opposed, but it's only because we've arranged society that the outcome is good.

Some of you may know about reinforcement learning — we have a direct map of this. Every agent has its own reward signal; those are its goals, like its food and its pain. Agents would normally have different rewards. But their *values* are predictions of reward (the technical term "value" in reinforcement learning means a prediction of reward). In a hopeful narrative, the values become aligned: even though I might want my food, I'd also recognize a principle that we respect each other's property and don't try to beat each other up or take things from each other. This is really how our world works — we've set up laws and mores so that a certain kind of behavior is rational; everyone gets the most reward by behaving appropriately and leaving space for others.

## One AI or many? The complex adaptive system

Another point, in the fearful narrative: is there one AI or many? The most obvious fearful narrative is that they'll become one super-AI that quickly becomes much more powerful than all the people and all the other AIs, takes over, and then you have a "singleton" — a single agent that controls our world. This relies on the idea that it's *fast*. If the takeoff is not fast, it makes more sense that there would be many. To connect it to the last point: we can't have peace if there's one strongman who can dominate everyone else. It's because no one is totally in control — everyone has to share power — that you get peace, and that's how you get aligned values in human societies. The same would be true among AIs.

I can't resist mentioning the idea of the **complex adaptive system**. My view is that the world is not something that's in control; it's a complex adaptive system — decentralized, many parts that have to share power with each other — and this is where it gets much of its robustness, its ability to shift and be dynamic, to find the way of being that's most successful. So part of the hopeful narrative is to view the world as a complex adaptive system: it would be very difficult to take over, and those who might try would actually end up poorly, so it would be irrational to try. A super-intelligent rational agent will be seeking to work with us and with the other AIs, rather than trying to take over.

## Summary

That's about all I really want to offer. Let's recognize that people's attitudes toward these questions will not change very rapidly. It's enough just to bring up the questions — to realize there *is* a standard narrative, that it may not be the only narrative, and that it may be leading us to a bad place. I urge each of you not to rush to assume that this fearful narrative is natural and inevitable.

To summarize the stories of AI: first, this is the century of computation, driven by Moore's law. Then there are two dimensions — tool AI and agent AI — each with positive and negative hype, each with a risk/reward profile. The risk/reward profile of tool AI is familiar and manageable, and it's often conflated with that of agent AI, which is of higher variance. Which narrative prevails — which meme becomes popular — is really important for the story of AI. And who sets that up? You all do. We set the narratives, we spread the memes, and it's something we should feel responsible for.

**Sutton:** I really am against centralized organizations. I'm against the World Economic Forum and all those others, so I wouldn't want to create another one. But I like sharing, I like being open, like open source. They've got to be challenged by others; you can't keep them secret, you have to publish. I think it's a bit of a problem that corporations are becoming more closed and less likely to publish now. As for intellectual property — without claiming deep understanding, I have a strong reaction that intellectual property is always a waste of time and counterproductive. It's costly, it slows things down, and it doesn't really protect in a good way. So I'm not interested in having intellectual property at all. Thank you for your question.
